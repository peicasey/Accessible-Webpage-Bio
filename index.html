Outlook on Accessibility

In the process of making my own personal website (not this one) last summer, I doom-scrolled browsed many websites that were just plain stunning to look at (see examples on godly.website and awwwards).

I wanted to emulate the amazing aesthetics, wow-factor, and appeal that these websites had to me. However, I realized that those things were often only able to be appreciated by those both with computers powerful enough to process massive amounts of JavaScript and also those who navigate the web in a certain un-universal way.

Yet I don't believe that design cannot both be accessible and appealing many. So my goals in accessibility are to get a more solid understanding of needs and methods to implement them. That way I am able to rework into both my previous and future projects, making designs that are available to everyone.

Attempts at making this specific website accessible include:

  1. Checking the color contrast of this design on Web Aim's Contrast Checker .
  2. Adding some header tags to switch between sections on a page.
  3. Having the header stick for easy navigation.
  4. Using aria-hidden to make sure decorative items are ignored by screen readers.
  5. Checking the tab-navigatability of my site.
  6. Using Atkinsons Hyperlegible.
  7. Added an accessibility widget for custom adjustments (though I would like to make my own for maximum compatiability).
  8. Put my site in dark-mode for reduced eye strain (though I would like to add an option for light-mode for others who experience less strain with a light-mode).
